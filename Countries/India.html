Communication

  • In India it's traditional to greet people using "Namaste" - place both hands together and bow slightly, however, in urban areas this is often overlooked.
  • Men will often also shake hands when meeting or leaving, but they should not attempt to shake hands with women.
  • Indian culture is based on a hierarchical system, so elders and superior are greeted first.
  • Use your right hand to touch, accept, or give something.
  • Do not beckon with your hand or snap your fingers instead, with your arm extended, curl your fingers downward in a claw motion.
  • Pointing, with either one or two fingers, is considered rude and used for inferiors only while chins, thumbs and entire hands are used to point or direct someone's attention.
  • Feet are considered unclean. Feet are sacred for holy men and women. Pointing footwear at people is considered an insult.
  • It is important to limit any public displays of affection because PDA can be seen as offensive especially to the older generations.

Table Manners

  • You may be invited to wash your hands before eating.
  • If hosts eat with hands, assure them you enjoy doing the same. If utensils are not used, use your right hand (first three fingers and thumb) to eat.
  • Many strict Muslims, and Hindu women, do not drink alcohol.
  • Guests are generally served in a hierarchical order: guest of honour, men, then children (women may eat later).
  • Try to not use your left hand for eating or talking to people around you.
  • A host will always serve their guests. Accept whatever you are offered but don't feel obliged to finish everything on your plate.
  • If food is placed in communal dishes for you to help yourself, always use a spoon.
  • In a restaurant, the host will generally pay the bill.
  • Arrive 15-30 minutes later than the stated time for a dinner party and take off shoes before entering the person's home.
  • Wait until everyone else is sitting down before starting to eat.

Miscellaneous

Tourist Attractions

  • Meenakshi Amman Temple: An impressive Hindu temple that dates back to the sixth century, though most of the present structure was built a thousand years later. The temple has a total of 14 towers each dedicated to a god or person, and it is one of the most important temples in Madurai.
  • Harmandir Sahib: The Golden Temple is the main tourist attraction in Amritsar, and the most important religious place to the Sikhs.
  • Taj Mahal: The Taj Mahal in Agra is an immense mausoleum of white marble, built between 1632 and 1653 by order of the Mughal emperor Shah Jahan in memory of his favorite wife. Called "a teardrop on the cheek of eternity" it is one of the masterpieces of Mughal architecture, and one of the great tourist attractions in India. Besides the white domed marble mausoleum the Taj Mahal includes several other beautiful buildings, reflecting pools, and extensive ornamental gardens with flowering trees and bushes.
  • Hawa Mahal: A stunning red sandstone five-story structure stands in the heart of Jaipur. One of the city's most popular tourist attractions, the Hawa Mahal has a unique purpose. Also known as the Palace of the Wind, it was a place where royal women could view street activities outside while hidden from view.
  • The Holy City of Varanasi: A major pilgrimage center for Hindus, the holy city of Varanasi has long been associated with the mighty Ganges River, one of the faith's most important religious symbols. Dating back to the 8th century BC, Varanasi is one of the oldest still inhabited cities in the world.
